### Link kernel and the weak-field Newtonian limit

The original local-density form of the resolution rule cannot by itself
describe gravitational redshift: redshift is controlled by a potential, not
only by the local matter density. We therefore replace the local rule by a
causal, non-local link kernel built from irreducible causal relations in the
underlying sprinkling.

Let \(y\prec x\) denote causal precedence, and let \(I(y,x)\) be the open
causal interval between \(y\) and \(x\). A pair \((y,x)\) is a link when no
sprinkled element lies inside \(I(y,x)\). The microscopic no-blocker selector
is

\[
\chi_{\rm link}(x,y)
=
\mathbf 1[y\prec x]\,
\mathbf 1\!\left[N(I(y,x))=0\right].
\]

For a Poisson sprinkling with density \(\rho_s(u)\), the expectation value of
the no-blocker condition is

\[
\langle\chi_{\rm link}(x,y)\rangle
=
\mathbf 1[y\prec x]\,
\exp\!\left[
-\int_{I(y,x)}\rho_s(u)\,dV_u
\right].
\]

We define the retarded link kernel

\[
K_{\rm ret}(x,y)
=
\mathcal N_R\,
\mathbf 1[y\prec x]\,
\exp\!\left[
-\int_{I(y,x)}\rho_s(u)\,dV_u
\right]\,
W_R(x,y),
\]

where \(W_R\) is a finite-resolution or finite-domain window and
\(\mathcal N_R\) fixes the continuum normalization. The auxiliary potential
generated by a physical source density \(\varrho(y)\) is

\[
\Psi(x)
=
\int K_{\rm ret}(x,y)\,\varrho(y)\,d^4y.
\]

Here \(\rho_s\) denotes the sprinkling density, while \(\varrho\) denotes the
physical source density. Keeping the two densities distinct is essential:
the first controls the causal-set sampling, the second sources the effective
field.

In the weak-field, static-source limit, the Newtonian comparison is not made
with the four-dimensional causal kernel pointwise. Instead, one first forms a
stationary projection,

\[
K_{\rm stat}(\mathbf x,\mathbf y)
=
\int d\Delta t\,
K_{\rm sym}\!\left((t,\mathbf x),(t+\Delta t,\mathbf y)\right),
\]

where \(K_{\rm sym}=(K_{\rm ret}(x,y)+K_{\rm ret}(y,x))/2\) is used only for
the projected static response. The weak-field target is then

\[
K_{\rm stat}(\mathbf x,\mathbf y)
\longrightarrow
-\frac{G_{\rm eff}}{|\mathbf x-\mathbf y|}
\]

over the calibrated range. Equivalently, the numerical diagnostic is

\[
r\,K_{\rm stat}(r)\simeq {\rm constant}.
\]

Thus \(K(x,y)\) is proposed as a causal-set generator of the Newtonian
potential only after static projection and weak-field calibration. The claim
is not that the microscopic four-dimensional kernel is itself \(1/r\), but
that its projected response can reproduce the Newtonian Green function in
the appropriate limit.

continuum Green functions can be recovered from order-theoretic data of a
causal set rather than inserted as continuum differential operators from the
start. The present use is narrower and project-specific. Here the link
kernel is used as a candidate generator of the Newtonian potential after
static projection, rather than as a generic scalar propagator.

## Guardrails

Safe:

1. "The projected link kernel is calibrated/tested against the Newtonian
   \(1/r\) response."
2. "Johnston provides precedent for causal-set propagators; the Newtonian use
   here is project-specific."
3. "The kernel has causal support; compact support requires the finite window
   \(W_R\) or a finite simulation domain."

Avoid:

1. "\(K(x,y)=1/r\)." The correct statement is about \(K_{\rm stat}\).
2. "Links prove Newtonian gravity." The correct statement is test/recovery in
   a weak-field limit.
3. "The kernel derives galaxy rotation curves." Galaxy phenomenology is a
   separate step.
4. "The support is compact" without naming \(W_R\) or a finite domain.
